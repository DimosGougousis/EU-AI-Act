1. Scope & Methodology

1.1 System in Scope

PulseCredit v2.1 — AI credit scoring and loan origination system used by FinPulse NL B.V. under its Consumer Credit Provider authorisation (Wft). Processes approximately 18,000 consumer loan applications per year for Dutch residents aged 18+.

1.2 Assessment Framework

Assessment conducted against the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (EUCFR), GDPR, and the Dutch Constitution. Methodology: structured rights inventory → impact assessment → mitigation design → residual risk sign-off.

Cross-referenced with GDPR DPIA-2025-003 (automated credit decision processing) conducted by the Data Protection Officer. FRIA extends DPIA findings to non-data-protection fundamental rights.

Right to Explanation & Effective Remedy GDPR Art. 22 (automated decision-making right); Art. 47 EUCFR (effective judicial remedy); Consumer Credit Directive Art. 9 Applicants who receive AI-influenced credit decisions have a legal right to an explanation and to contest the decision. Without meaningful explanation, this right is effectively denied. Automated decisions (≤€5k loans) are particularly at risk. High High
  • SHAP-based reason codes: top 3 factors communicated to loan officer
  • Plain-language rejection letter templates with factor-based explanation
  • Disclosure of AI use in all credit decision communications
  • Complaints & dispute process: internal review within 5 business days
  • Human review available on request for all automated decisions

3. GDPR DPIA Alignment

DPIA FindingFRIA Incorporation
DPIA-2025-003 confirmed: PulseCredit constitutes automated decision-making under GDPR Art. 22 for loans ≤€5k. GDPR Art. 22(2)(a) applies — automated decision is necessary for performance of a contract. FRIA extends this finding to the right-to-explanation dimension: Art. 22(3) requires suitable measures to safeguard rights, including right to obtain human intervention and to contest the decision. All measures documented in Human Oversight Design (Artifact 09).
DPIA found: processing of ethnic origin data (nationality as proxy) for bias testing falls under Art. 9(2)(g) substantial public interest, with appropriate safeguards (aggregated data only, deleted after assessment). FRIA confirms this safeguard is proportionate to the non-discrimination objective. The alternative (not testing for bias) would create higher fundamental rights risk.
DPIA recommends: data retention policy for loan applications aligned to 6-year consumer credit legal minimum. FRIA confirms retention policy is proportionate and aligns with EU AI Act Art. 12 logging obligations.
